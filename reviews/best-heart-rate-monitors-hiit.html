Buying Guide: How to Choose a Heart Rate Monitor for HIIT

1. Chest Strap vs Armband vs Wrist

Chest Straps: Most accurate, especially during HIIT when heart rate changes rapidly. Gold standard for serious training. Takes getting used to but provides medical-grade accuracy.

Armbands: More comfortable than chest straps, nearly as accurate. Optical sensors on forearm are better than wrist-based monitors. Great middle ground.

Wrist-Based (Watches): Most convenient but least accurate during HIIT. Rapid movements and wrist flexion cause errors. Fine for casual tracking, not ideal for zone-based HIIT training.

2. Connectivity: Bluetooth vs ANT+

Most modern heart rate monitors support both Bluetooth and ANT+. Bluetooth connects to phones and apps. ANT+ connects to gym equipment and Garmin watches. For maximum compatibility, get a monitor with both (like Polar H10 or Wahoo TICKR).

3. Battery Life Matters

Chest straps with replaceable coin batteries last 300-500 hours (6-12 months of daily HIIT). Rechargeable armbands last 8-24 hours per charge. For convenience, replaceable batteries win - no need to remember to charge before workouts.

4. Why Accuracy Matters for HIIT

HIIT training relies on hitting specific heart rate zones (80-95% max HR during work intervals). Inaccurate monitors can show you're in zone 4 when you're actually in zone 2, making your workout less effective. Chest straps provide ±1 bpm accuracy vs ±5-10 bpm for wrist monitors during intense exercise.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a heart rate monitor for HIIT workouts?

Not required, but highly recommended. A heart rate monitor ensures you're actually hitting high-intensity zones (80-95% max HR) during work intervals and recovering properly during rest. Without one, you might be working too hard (risking burnout) or not hard enough (limiting results). Our free HIIT timer works great with or without a monitor.

Are chest strap heart rate monitors more accurate than watches?

Yes, significantly. Chest straps measure electrical signals from your heart (ECG) with ±1 bpm accuracy. Wrist-based optical sensors struggle during HIIT due to movement, sweat, and wrist flexion, often showing ±5-10 bpm errors. For zone-based HIIT training, chest straps are the gold standard.

Can I use my Apple Watch or Fitbit for HIIT heart rate tracking?

Yes, but with limitations. Wrist-based monitors are less accurate during intense exercise. For casual HIIT tracking, they work fine. For serious zone-based training, pair your watch with a chest strap (most support external HR monitors via Bluetooth).
